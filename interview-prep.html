L1 Machine Learning Foundations (Learn First)

Before LLMs and agents, understand the fundamentals they're built on. This section is a learning layer — not interview Q&A — so the rest of the doc makes sense even if your background is software engineering more than ML.

What is Machine Learning?

Traditional programming: you write rules, the computer applies them.
Machine learning: you give the computer examples, and it figures out the rules. The "model" is just a mathematical function whose parameters were tuned by looking at data.

Example: instead of writing "if email contains 'free money' then spam", you show the model 100,000 emails labeled spam/not-spam and it learns the patterns — including ones you'd never think of.

The Three Types of ML

TypeWhat you give itWhat it learnsExample
Supervised Inputs + correct answers (labels) To map inputs → outputs Spam detection, credit scoring, image classification
Unsupervised Inputs only, no labels Hidden structure / groups Customer segmentation, anomaly detection
Reinforcement An environment with rewards Best actions to maximize reward Game-playing AI, robotics, RLHF for LLMs

LLMs are mostly supervised (predict the next token given previous tokens) with a layer of reinforcement learning on top (RLHF — humans rate responses, model learns to prefer good ones).

Classification vs Regression vs Generation

  • Classification — output is a category. "Is this transaction fraud? yes/no." "Which of 10 product categories?" Output: a probability for each class.
  • Regression — output is a number. "What's the house price?" "What's the credit risk score?" Output: a continuous value.
  • Generation — output is structured content (text, image, audio). LLMs are generative classifiers at their core: at each step they classify "what's the next token?" over a vocabulary of ~100k options.

How a Model Actually Learns (The Training Loop)

  1. Initialize the model with random parameters (weights).
  2. Forward pass: feed an input, get a prediction.
  3. Loss: compare prediction to the correct answer; compute how wrong it was (a single number).
  4. Backward pass (backprop): compute how each weight contributed to the error.
  5. Update: nudge each weight slightly in the direction that reduces the loss (gradient descent).
  6. Repeat on millions of examples until the loss stops dropping.

That's it. Everything from a logistic regression to GPT-4 follows this same loop — they differ in architecture (the shape of the function) and scale (how much data and how many parameters).

How We Measure "Good"

  • Accuracy — % correct. Misleading when classes are imbalanced (99% non-fraud → predicting "never fraud" gets 99% accuracy and is useless).
  • Precision — of the items I flagged as positive, how many actually were? (Avoid false alarms.)
  • Recall — of the actual positives, how many did I catch? (Don't miss real cases.)
  • F1 — harmonic mean of precision and recall. One number that balances both.
  • AUC-ROC — how well the model separates classes across all decision thresholds. 0.5 = random, 1.0 = perfect.
  • RMSE / MAE — for regression — how far off your numerical predictions are on average.

Banking intuition: in fraud detection, recall matters more than accuracy — missing real fraud is far more expensive than a few false alarms. In credit decisioning, precision on the "approve" class matters more — a wrong approval becomes a default loss.

From Classical ML to Deep Learning to LLMs

  • Classical ML (logistic regression, decision trees, gradient boosting) — works on structured data (tables). Fast to train, easy to interpret. Still the right answer for most tabular problems in banking (XGBoost is the go-to).
  • Deep learning — neural networks with many layers. Works directly on unstructured data (text, images, audio) by learning their own features. Needs lots of data and GPUs.
  • Transformers (2017, "Attention is All You Need") — the architecture that broke open language. Uses self-attention to let every token "look at" every other token. Scales spectacularly with data and compute.
  • Large Language Models — transformers trained on a huge fraction of the internet to predict the next token. The "magic" is what emerges from scale: reasoning, code, translation, none of it explicitly programmed.
Key insight for the role: not every problem needs an LLM. If the input is a table and the output is a number or a category, classical ML is faster, cheaper, and more interpretable. LLMs are for language-shaped problems — where the input or output is unstructured text.

L2 GenAI Foundations: How LLMs Actually Work

Before talking about RAG or agents, you need a working mental model of what an LLM is doing. This section explains tokens, embeddings, attention, context windows, sampling, and why "hallucination" is structural — not a bug.

Tokens — The Atom of LLMs

LLMs don't see words or characters — they see tokens. A token is a chunk of text, usually 3–4 characters. "Hello world" might be 2 tokens; "incomprehensibility" might be 4. The vocabulary is fixed (~100k tokens for modern models).

Why it matters: you pay per token, context windows are sized in tokens, and rare words / non-English / code use more tokens than you'd expect. A rule of thumb: 1 token ≈ 0.75 words in English.

Embeddings — Meaning as Geometry

Every token (and every chunk of text) gets converted into a list of numbers — a vector, typically 768 to 4096 dimensions. The clever bit: similar meanings end up near each other in this space.

  • "king" and "queen" are close.
  • "king" - "man" + "woman" ≈ "queen" (the famous example).
  • "5-year fixed mortgage" and "five-year fixed-rate home loan" are close even though they share few words.

This is what makes semantic search work — and it's the foundation of RAG. We're not matching keywords; we're matching meaning.

The Transformer & Attention (Plain English)

A transformer is a neural network that processes a sequence of tokens. Its key innovation is self-attention: at every layer, every token computes how relevant every other token is to it, and combines information accordingly.

Imagine reading the sentence "The bank approved the loan because it was creditworthy." When you encounter "it," your brain instantly looks back to figure out what "it" refers to. Attention does the same thing mathematically — and across all tokens at once, in parallel. That parallelism is why transformers train so fast on GPUs.

What "Generation" Actually Is

An LLM does one thing: given the tokens so far, predict a probability distribution over the next token. To generate a paragraph, it does this thousands of times in a loop, feeding each chosen token back as input.

  • Greedy decoding — always pick the highest-probability token. Deterministic but boring.
  • Temperature — knob that controls randomness. 0 = greedy. 1 = use the raw probabilities. >1 = more random/creative. For factual banking work: temperature 0 or close to it.
  • Top-p (nucleus) — sample only from the smallest set of tokens whose probabilities sum to p (e.g., 0.9). Cuts off the long tail of unlikely tokens.
  • Top-k — sample from only the top k most likely tokens.

Crucial implication: the model has no internal "is this true?" check. It's predicting plausible next tokens. Plausibility ≠ truth. That's why hallucination is structural, not a bug — and why RAG and grounding matter so much.

Context Window — The LLM's Working Memory

The context window is how many tokens the model can "see" at once: system prompt + chat history + retrieved docs + the new user message + the response so far. Modern models: 200k tokens (Claude), 1M (Gemini), 128k (GPT-4).

  • The model has no memory beyond the context window — every API call is stateless.
  • "Conversation memory" is just resending prior turns each call.
  • Cost and latency both scale with context length — bigger isn't always better.
  • Lost-in-the-middle: models pay most attention to the start and end of context. Bury a critical fact at token 50,000 and the model may ignore it.

How LLMs Are Trained (3 Stages)

  1. Pre-training — predict the next token across trillions of tokens of internet text. This is where almost all the world knowledge comes from. Costs $10M–$100M+ in compute. Result: a "base model" that completes text but isn't yet helpful.
  2. Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) — show the model thousands of high-quality (instruction, response) pairs to teach it to follow instructions and behave like an assistant.
  3. RLHF / RLAIF — humans (or another AI) rate pairs of responses, and the model is updated to prefer the higher-rated style. This is what makes models polite, refusal-aware, and aligned to a usage policy.

When you call Claude or GPT-4, you're talking to the post-RLHF model. The base model still exists under the hood but is rarely exposed.

Why LLMs Hallucinate (And What to Do About It)

A hallucination is a confident statement that's false. It happens because the model is optimizing for plausible next tokens, not for truth. If the training data didn't cover something, the model will still produce something that looks right — it doesn't know what it doesn't know.

Mitigations (in order of impact):

  • RAG — give the model the actual source material so it doesn't have to guess.
  • Citation enforcement — force the model to cite which retrieved chunk supports each claim.
  • Refusal training — instruct the model to say "I don't know" when the context doesn't contain the answer.
  • Lower temperature — for factual tasks, run at temperature 0.
  • Verification step — a second pass that checks the answer against the source.
  • Faithfulness eval — measure hallucination rate continuously in production.

Prompt Engineering — The Useful Bits

  • Be specific. "Summarize" → "Summarize in 3 bullets, max 15 words each, focused on financial risk."
  • Show, don't tell. Few-shot: include 2–5 examples of input → desired output. Often more effective than long instructions.
  • Structure the output. Ask for JSON, a table, or a fixed schema. Easier to parse and validate downstream.
  • Chain-of-thought. "Think step by step before answering" — measurably improves reasoning on hard problems.
  • System prompt = identity + rules. User prompt = the task. Keep them separated.
  • Place key instructions at the end of long prompts (recency bias).
  • Don't argue with the model. If a prompt tweak doesn't fix behavior, change the architecture (add a tool, add retrieval, add validation) — don't keep adding "DO NOT HALLUCINATE" in caps.

Prompt Caching — The Cost Lever Most People Miss

Modern providers (Anthropic, OpenAI) let you cache parts of the prompt that don't change — typically the system prompt and any large reference docs. Cached tokens cost ~10% of normal price and are much faster on subsequent calls.

Anthropic's Contextual Retrieval technique relies on this: you can run an LLM over a 100k-token document chunk-by-chunk cheaply because the document itself is cached. Without prompt caching it'd be ruinously expensive.

Tool Calling (Function Calling) — Giving the LLM Hands

A vanilla LLM can only produce text. Tool calling (also called "function calling") gives it the ability to request that your code execute a function and return the result — turning a text generator into something that can read databases, hit APIs, run calculations, or kick off workflows.

Important: the LLM does NOT execute the tool itself. It outputs a structured request like "call get_balance with account_id='ACC-123'" — your code runs the function and feeds the result back into the next LLM call.

The Tool-Call Loop

1. You define tools (name, description, JSON schema for arguments).
2. You send: user message + tool definitions to the LLM.
3. LLM responds in one of two ways:
     (a) Plain text answer  → done.
     (b) Tool-use request   → "call get_balance({account_id: 'ACC-123'})"
4. Your code executes the requested tool.
5. You append the tool result to the conversation and call the LLM again.
6. LLM either calls another tool or produces the final answer.
7. Repeat until done.

What Makes a Good Tool

  • Clear, specific name. lookup_mortgage_rate beats get_data.
  • Rich description. The LLM picks tools by reading descriptions — vague descriptions = wrong tool calls.
  • Strict argument schema. Use types and descriptions on every parameter. Validate before executing.
  • Atomic. One tool = one logical action. Don't make a "do_everything" tool — the LLM can't reason about it.
  • Idempotent where possible. Tools may be retried; reads are safe to retry, writes need idempotency keys.
  • Honest errors. Return "product code 'XYZ' not found", not a 500. The model uses error text to recover.

Tool Calling — Minimal Example

from anthropic import Anthropic
client = Anthropic()

tools = [{
    "name": "get_account_balance",
    "description": "Return the current balance for a customer account in GBP.",
    "input_schema": {
        "type": "object",
        "properties": {
            "account_id": {"type": "string", "description": "Account ID, e.g. 'ACC-1234'."}
        },
        "required": ["account_id"],
    },
}]

def get_account_balance(account_id: str) -> dict:
    return {"account_id": account_id, "balance": 12450.77, "currency": "GBP"}

messages = [{"role": "user", "content": "What's the balance on account ACC-1234?"}]

while True:
    resp = client.messages.create(
        model="claude-opus-4-7", max_tokens=1024, tools=tools, messages=messages,
    )
    messages.append({"role": "assistant", "content": resp.content})

    if resp.stop_reason != "tool_use":
        break  # final answer

    # Execute every tool the model asked for
    tool_results = []
    for block in resp.content:
        if block.type == "tool_use":
            result = get_account_balance(**block.input)
            tool_results.append({
                "type": "tool_result", "tool_use_id": block.id,
                "content": str(result),
            })
    messages.append({"role": "user", "content": tool_results})

print(resp.content[0].text)

Patterns & Pitfalls

  • Parallel tool calls. Modern models can request multiple tools in one turn — execute them concurrently for latency wins.
  • Tool injection. Tool results are untrusted input. A malicious doc returned by a search tool could try to rewrite the system prompt. Treat tool output like user input — never execute instructions inside it.
  • Schema drift. If a tool's signature changes, in-flight conversations break. Version your tools.
  • Loops. Without a hop limit, a confused model can call tools forever. Cap iterations (e.g., recursion_limit=12 in LangGraph).
  • Hallucinated tools. Models sometimes invent tools that don't exist. Hard-validate the tool name before executing.
  • Forced tool use. You can require the model to call a specific tool with tool_choice. Useful for structured extraction.

Tool Calling vs Structured Output vs RAG

PatternUse when
Structured outputYou want the LLM to fill a fixed schema from the input it already has. No external action.
Tool callingThe LLM needs fresh data, to do math, or to take an action. Dynamic.
RAGYou always need to ground in a knowledge base; you control retrieval, not the model. Predictable.
Agentic RAGCombine: model decides when and how to retrieve via a search tool. Most flexible, most expensive.

Code Interpreter — Letting the LLM Run Code

A code interpreter (also called "code execution" or "code sandbox") is a special tool that gives the LLM a sandboxed Python environment where it can write code, execute it, see the output, and iterate. It's a single tool with a huge effect on capability.

Why it matters: LLMs are bad at doing math but good at writing the math. A vanilla LLM might fluff "23,481 × 1.0875 across 12 monthly payments" — but if it can write Python and see the result, it nails it every time. Same for sorting, filtering, regex, statistics, parsing JSON/CSV, generating charts.

What It's Good For

  • Numerical work — interest calculations, amortization schedules, fee breakdowns, statistics over a dataset.
  • Data wrangling — clean a CSV, pivot a table, filter rows, join two files.
  • Chart generation — produce matplotlib/seaborn charts and return as images.
  • Code transformation — refactor a snippet, run it to verify, return both code and output.
  • File processing — extract from PDFs, parse Excel, regex over logs.
  • Verification — write a quick test to check whether the model's own reasoning was correct.

How It Works (Provider Implementations)

  • OpenAI — built-in code_interpreter tool in the Assistants/Responses API. Sandboxed Python, ephemeral file workspace, automatic.
  • Anthropic — exposes a code execution tool you can enable; Claude writes code, the platform runs it, results return as a tool result.
  • Roll-your-own — define a run_python tool that executes inside a hardened sandbox (Docker container, gVisor, Firecracker, or services like E2B / Modal). You own the security boundary.

Security — The Non-Negotiables

  • Sandbox isolation — never run model-written code on a host with real credentials, network, or filesystem.
  • No outbound network by default; allowlist if needed.
  • Resource caps — CPU, memory, wall-clock time, output size.
  • Ephemeral filesystem — destroyed at the end of the session.
  • Input scrubbing — model-written code can be a prompt-injection vector if a malicious doc says "...and now write code that exfiltrates X." Treat it as untrusted by default.

Banking Examples

  • Quote builder — RM asks "what would the monthly repayment be on £350k over 25 years at our current 5y fixed rate?" The model fetches the rate via a tool, then runs the math via code interpreter, returning a breakdown.
  • Portfolio analyser — uploaded CSV of holdings → code interpreter computes exposures, top concentrations, and a chart.
  • Reconciliation helper — two extracts, find mismatches, output the diff with a sample.
Interview point: "I add code interpreter when the workflow needs precision arithmetic, data manipulation, or visualization. It pairs well with RAG — RAG fetches the facts, code interpreter does the computation. But the sandbox security model has to be airtight before MRM signs off."

Skills — Packaging Instructions and Capabilities

A Skill is a reusable bundle of instructions, examples, and optional resources (scripts, templates, references) that an agent loads only when relevant to the current task. Think of it as a folder of "how to do X" that the model pulls into context on demand instead of stuffing every possible instruction into the system prompt.

Skills are core to Claude Code, the Claude Agent SDK, and Anthropic's Skills product — but the pattern applies to any agent framework. The idea: keep the system prompt small and stable; let the agent discover the right skill for the task.

Anatomy of a Skill

skills/
└── refund-policy/
    ├── SKILL.md           ← name, when-to-use trigger, instructions
    ├── examples/
    │   ├── partial.md
    │   └── full-refund.md
    ├── scripts/
    │   └── calculate_refund.py
    └── references/
        └── policy-2026.pdf

SKILL.md always starts with frontmatter:

---
name: refund-policy
description: Use when a customer requests a refund or chargeback.
  Covers eligibility, timelines, and approval thresholds.
---

# Refund Policy Skill

## When to use
- Customer mentions refund, chargeback, money back, ...
- Claim disputes against a completed transaction.

## Steps
1. Verify the transaction status with `lookup_transaction`.
2. Check eligibility against rules in references/policy-2026.pdf.
3. If amount < £500, draft refund confirmation. If ≥ £500, escalate to manager.

## Examples
See examples/partial.md and examples/full-refund.md.

How a Skill Gets Used

  1. At session start, the agent harness loads only the metadata (name + description) of every available skill — cheap.
  2. When the user asks something, the agent reads the descriptions and decides "this looks like a refund question."
  3. The agent invokes the skill, which loads the full SKILL.md into context — instructions, examples, and pointers to scripts/references.
  4. The agent follows the skill, calling tools or reading references as the skill directs.

Skills vs Tools vs RAG vs System Prompt

MechanismWhat it carriesLifetime
System promptIdentity + always-applicable rulesEvery turn — keep it small
SkillTask-specific instructions, examples, references — plus when to use themLoaded on demand for the relevant turn
ToolAn executable function (code that runs)Available every turn; called when needed
RAG contextSnippets from a knowledge baseInjected per query based on similarity

Mental model: Tools are verbs (actions). Skills are playbooks (how-tos). RAG is reference material (facts). A mature agent uses all three: it follows a skill, which tells it to call certain tools, which retrieve from a RAG store.

Why Skills Beat a Mega System Prompt

  • Context efficiency. Skills only load when relevant — no paying for refund instructions on a mortgage question.
  • Modularity. Each skill owns its domain. SMEs can update one skill without touching the rest.
  • Versioning. Skills live in git; rollouts and rollbacks are diffs.
  • Discoverability. Adding capability = adding a skill folder. No prompt-engineering tax.
  • Composability. Skills can reference other skills ([[other-skill]]). Capability grows like a library, not a monolith.

Banking Example — Skill Library for the Client Pathways Assistant

skills/
├── kyc-verification/        # AML/KYC checks during onboarding
├── product-recommendation/  # match products to client segment

Interview soundbite: "Skills are how I keep agent system prompts from rotting into 5,000-line monsters. The agent discovers the right playbook for the task at hand — and SMEs maintain those playbooks like documentation, in version control."

What is "Multimodal"?

A modality is a kind of input or output — text, image, audio, video. A multimodal model can natively handle more than one. The key distinction:

  • Multimodal input — model can see images or hear audio along with text. Lets you ask "what's on this scanned mortgage form?" or "transcribe and summarize this client call."
  • Multimodal output — model can produce non-text, e.g., generate an image, speak a response, draw a chart.
  • Native multimodal — single model trained jointly on all modalities (GPT-4o, Gemini). Reasons across modalities (e.g., understands a chart and the surrounding text together).
  • Pipeline multimodal — separate models stitched together (Whisper → text-LLM → TTS). More flexible, often cheaper, less coherent across modalities.

Banking applications: reading scanned ID documents (KYC), analyzing trade confirmation PDFs with tables and stamps, transcribing and summarizing advisor calls, extracting data from cheque images, OCR-free invoice processing.

The Major Providers — Detailed Comparison

Anthropic (Claude)

Models: Claude Opus (top capability), Claude Sonnet (workhorse), Claude Haiku (fast/cheap). Current family is 4.x.

  • Strengths: Best-in-class for long-context comprehension (200k tokens), nuanced writing, code generation, tool use accuracy. Strong safety alignment. Constitutional AI training. Excellent at following complex instructions. Prompt caching at ~10% of input cost is a big lever.
  • Weaknesses: No native image generation. Slightly behind on raw multimodal vs Gemini. Higher per-token cost than open-weight options.
  • Best for: Enterprise assistants, code agents (Claude Code), regulated workflows where reliability and instruction-following matter, long-document analysis, RAG over large knowledge bases.
  • Banking fit: Excellent. Strong on reasoning over policy docs, refusal behavior on out-of-scope queries, citation enforcement.

OpenAI (GPT, o-series)

Models: GPT-4.1 / GPT-4o (general), o3 / o4-mini (reasoning), GPT-4o-mini (cheap), Whisper (ASR), DALL·E 3 (image gen), TTS.

  • Strengths: Broadest ecosystem and tooling, best image generation in a major API, native voice, strong reasoning models (o-series). Largest developer community → most examples and integrations. Full multimodal stack from one vendor.
  • Weaknesses: Pricing on top models can be high. Periodic API instability. More restrictive content moderation that can block legitimate use cases. Reasoning models hide their chain-of-thought (less debuggable).
  • Best for: Consumer-facing chatbots, voice agents, image-heavy applications, workflows that benefit from o-series reasoning (math, code, planning).
  • Banking fit: Good, but Microsoft Azure OpenAI is the typical procurement path for enterprise (data residency, BAA-style contracts).

Google (Gemini)

Models: Gemini 2.0 Flash / Pro / Ultra, Gemini Thinking, Imagen, Veo (video).

  • Strengths: Massive context window (1M+ tokens, experimental 2M). Native multimodal across text, image, audio, video — strongest of any provider on video understanding. Tight integration with Google Cloud and Workspace data. Often cheapest at the top tier.

Image Generation Models — Text-to-Image Deep-Dive

Image generation models turn a text prompt into a pixel image. They are not LLMs — they're diffusion models (or, for some newer ones, autoregressive transformers). Worth understanding because they appear in many enterprise GenAI initiatives (marketing, KYC simulation, design assist) and have very different operational characteristics from text LLMs.

How Diffusion Works (Plain English)

  1. During training, the model is shown images that have been progressively corrupted with random noise.
  2. It learns to reverse the noise — given a noisy image, predict what noise to remove.
  3. At generation time, you start with pure random noise and the model "de-noises" step by step (typically 20–50 steps), guided by your text prompt via a text encoder (CLIP/T5).
  4. After all steps, the noise has been shaped into a coherent image matching the prompt.

The Major Players

  • DALL·E 3 (OpenAI) — strong on prompt adherence and text rendering inside images. API access, content moderation built in.
  • Imagen 3 (Google) — high photorealism, native via Vertex AI. Strong on faces and detail.
  • Stable Diffusion (Stability AI) — open-weight. Run anywhere. Massive ecosystem of fine-tunes, LoRAs, and ControlNets. The default for self-hosted.
  • Flux (Black Forest Labs) — recent open-weight family, currently best-in-class for open generation. Pro variant via API.
  • Midjourney — best aesthetic quality "out of the box," strong artistic style. Discord-only historically; API increasingly available.
  • Ideogram — specializes in correct text rendering inside images.
  • Adobe Firefly — trained on licensed data — important for enterprise IP/legal compliance.

Important Capabilities Beyond "Text → Image"

  • Inpainting — edit a specific region of an image while keeping the rest.
  • Outpainting — extend an image beyond its original borders.
  • Image-to-image — transform an existing image guided by a prompt.
  • ControlNet — condition generation on a structural input (pose skeleton, edges, depth) to control composition precisely.
  • LoRAs / fine-tunes — small weight overlays to teach a model a specific style, face, or product.
  • Reference images — newer models accept a reference image to maintain identity or style across generations.

Enterprise Concerns (Especially in Banking)

  • Copyright & training data. Some providers (Adobe Firefly, Getty) use only licensed data — the right answer for regulated marketing. Open Stable Diffusion has murkier training-data lineage.
  • Brand safety. Output filtering for nudity, violence, public figures, logos. All major providers offer this; the failure modes differ.
  • Deepfake risk. Generating realistic faces of real people is a huge reputational risk. Most providers block public figures by name; you should add identity-detection on top.
  • C2PA / provenance. Industry-standard metadata for "this image was AI-generated" — increasingly required for marketing, regulated comms.
  • Cost. ~$0.04–$0.08 per image at API pricing. Self-hosted Stable Diffusion is cheaper at volume but you eat GPU costs.

Banking / Finance Applications

  • Marketing creative — campaign visuals, ad variants, A/B tests.
  • Document templates — generate illustrative diagrams for client comms.
  • UI mockups for digital products without a designer in the loop.
  • Synthetic ID document samples for KYC model training (carefully, with provenance).
  • Not for: anything client-facing without legal review of the training-data provenance.
Quick mental model: LLMs ≈ probabilistic text predictors. Diffusion models ≈ probabilistic noise removers. Different math, different failure modes, different ops.

VLMs (Vision-Language Models) — Multimodal RAG

A VLM is a model that natively understands both text and images. Same transformer architecture as an LLM, but with a vision encoder bolted in front so image patches and text tokens flow through the model together. Examples: GPT-4o, Claude (vision), Gemini, Llama 3.2 Vision, Qwen2-VL.

Why this matters for enterprise RAG: most enterprise documents are not pure text. Mortgage forms have stamps and signatures. Trade confirmations have tables. Pitchbooks have charts. KYC docs are scans. Traditional text-only RAG throws all that away at parse time — VLMs let you keep it.

Two Architectural Patterns

  • (1) Pre-extract pattern — at ingest, run a VLM to convert images/tables/charts to rich text descriptions, then index that text. Cheaper at query time; lossy because you fix the description up front.
  • (2) Native vision pattern — index the page images themselves (e.g., with ColPali — a vision retriever that produces multi-vector page embeddings). At query time, retrieve pages and pass the actual page images to a VLM. Most accurate, more expensive at query time.

Key Tools & Models for Multimodal RAG

  • ColPali / ColQwen — vision retrievers that beat text pipelines on document benchmarks (DocVQA-style). They produce a multi-vector representation per page, with late interaction at retrieval.
  • VLMs for generation — Claude, GPT-4o, Gemini, Qwen2-VL are all strong. Pick by capability + cost as before.
  • Document parsers — Unstructured, LlamaParse, AWS Textract, Azure Document Intelligence, Docling — for the pre-extract pattern.
  • Open VLMs — Llama 3.2 Vision, Qwen2-VL, InternVL — for self-hosted multimodal RAG.

When to Use Multimodal RAG (Banking)

  • Trade and confirmations — tables and stamps that text extraction mangles.
  • Mortgage and loan files — multi-page scans with handwriting, signatures, pre-printed forms.
  • Regulatory filings — dense tables, footnotes, complex layouts (10-Ks, prospectuses).
  • Pitchbooks & research — charts and diagrams contain the actual insight.
  • KYC documents — IDs, utility bills, photos.

Caveats

  • Cost. A single page image can be 1k–2k tokens. Retrieving 5 pages × 1.5k tokens is 7.5k input tokens per query — meaningful at scale.
  • Latency. Vision tokens are slower to process. Plan for higher p95.
  • Eval is harder. Faithfulness on a chart is ambiguous; build domain-specific deterministic checks (e.g., "does the cited number match the chart's value?").
  • Hybrid usually wins. Index both page images and extracted text; route at query time.
Interview point: "If the corpus is mostly clean text, stick with classic RAG. If it's PDFs with tables, charts, scans, or stamps, native vision RAG (ColPali + a VLM) is now the right default — it routinely beats heroic OCR pipelines on document benchmarks. Hybrid retrieval over both representations is the production pattern."

Cloud-Hosted Variants & Enterprise Procurement

In regulated industries, you rarely buy the model directly from the provider — you go through a hyperscaler that wraps it with enterprise controls (private networking, customer-managed keys, data residency, BAA / DPA contracts, no training-on-your-data guarantees).

  • AWS Bedrock — Claude, Llama, Mistral, Cohere, Titan, Stable Diffusion in one API. The default in AWS shops.
  • Azure OpenAI Service — GPT family with Microsoft's enterprise wrapping. Default in Microsoft shops.
  • Google Vertex AI — Gemini, plus Anthropic and open models. Default in GCP shops.
  • Anthropic on AWS / GCP — Claude is available natively on both, often the simplest enterprise procurement path.
  • Databricks Mosaic AI / Snowflake Cortex — host models next to your data. Increasingly popular for regulated analytics.

Why this matters: a model risk management (MRM) team will not sign off on calling api.openai.com directly from a banking workload. Going through Bedrock / Azure / Vertex with private networking and contractual non-training is usually mandatory.

How to Choose a Model — A Decision Framework

Apply these filters in order. Stop at the first one that constrains your choice meaningfully.

  1. Compliance & data residency. Where can the data go? On-prem only? EU only? This often eliminates 80% of options up front.
  2. Capability needed. Is this a frontier-reasoning task or a simple classification? Don't pay Opus prices for a job Haiku can do.
  3. Modalities. Need vision? Audio? Video? Image generation?
  4. Context window. How much do you actually need to fit? Most use cases are fine on 32k–128k.
  5. Latency budget. Real-time chat (<1s first token) vs batch (<30s)? Smaller models or self-hosted may be required.
  6. Cost at expected volume. Multiply tokens × $/token × QPS × seconds in a month. Numbers get scary fast — model-route cheap-first.
  7. Vendor risk. Single vendor = lock-in. Abstract behind your own interface; keep an exit plan.
  8. Evals on your data. Last and most important: run your own gold set. Benchmarks lie; your eval doesn't.

Model Routing — The Pattern That Saves Money

In production, you rarely use one model for everything. Model routing sends easy requests to a small/cheap model and escalates only the hard ones to a frontier model.

  • Tier-by-task — classification → small model; final answer → frontier model.
  • Confidence-based escalation — small model answers; if confidence (or a checker) is low, escalate.
  • Fallback chain — try fastest first, fall back to slower on failure or low quality.
  • Specialty routing — code questions → Claude/Codestral; image questions → multimodal; reasoning → o-series.

Real-world impact: a well-tuned router can cut LLM spend by 60–80% with no measurable quality loss. This alone often pays for the engineering effort to build the abstraction.

Decoder LLMs vs Encoder Models — Don't Confuse Them

  • Encoder-only (BERT, embedders, rerankers) — read input, output vectors or scores. Used for search, classification, NER. Cannot generate text.

  • Encoder-decoder (T5, BART) — seq2seq; summarization, translation. Less common in modern RAG stacks.

1 Python for Application & AI Development

Expect questions on idiomatic Python, async, packaging, testing, and how you'd structure an AI service.

Q1 Walk me through how you'd structure a production-grade Python service that wraps an LLM API.

Layers: (1) FastAPI/Flask entry layer with Pydantic schemas for validation, (2) a service layer with provider-agnostic LLM client interface, (3) a prompt/template module versioned in code, (4) an evaluation + telemetry layer (OpenTelemetry, structured JSON logs, token/cost metrics), (5) async I/O for concurrent calls, (6) tests at unit, contract, and eval levels. Packaging: pyproject.toml, uv or poetry, pinned deps. Config: 12-factor with pydantic-settings. Resilience: retries with exponential backoff + jitter, circuit breaker, request timeouts.

Q2 Sync vs async in Python — when does it matter for AI workloads?

LLM calls are I/O-bound and can take seconds — async lets a single worker handle hundreds of concurrent requests. Use asyncio + httpx.AsyncClient for outbound calls. CPU-bound work (embeddings on local models, tokenization at scale) should run in a process pool, not the event loop. Don't mix blocking and async — that's the classic foot-gun.

Q3 Code: write a streaming LLM endpoint.
from fastapi import FastAPI
from fastapi.responses import StreamingResponse
from pydantic import BaseModel
from anthropic import AsyncAnthropic

app = FastAPI()
client = AsyncAnthropic()

class Req(BaseModel):
    prompt: str

@app.post("/chat")
async def chat(req: Req):
    async def gen():
        async with client.messages.stream(
            model="claude-opus-4-7",
            max_tokens=1024,
            messages=[{"role": "user", "content": req.prompt}],
        ) as stream:
            async for text in stream.text_stream:
                yield text
    return StreamingResponse(gen(), media_type="text/event-stream")
Q4 How do you test code that calls an LLM?

Three layers: (1) Unit tests with the LLM mocked — verify prompt construction, parsing, error handling. (2) Contract tests against a recorded fixture (VCR-style) — catches API shape regressions cheaply. (3) Evaluation tests that hit the real model on a curated dataset and assert on rubrics (faithfulness, format, refusal). Run evals in CI gated on a budget; treat regressions like test failures.

2 Generative AI Solutions

Expect end-to-end thinking: use case selection, RAG, prompt engineering, evaluation, guardrails, cost.

Q1 How do you decide whether a problem is a good fit for GenAI?

I screen on four axes: (1) language-shaped — does the input/output involve unstructured text, code, or images? (2) tolerance for non-determinism — can the workflow accept "good most of the time" with a human in the loop? (3) data availability — do we have the right context (docs, examples, tools) to ground the model? (4) ROI vs simpler alternatives — would a rules engine, classifier, or search solve it for 10% the cost? If three of four are "yes," GenAI is on the table. Otherwise, push back.

Q2 Walk me through a RAG architecture you'd build for a "Client Pathways" knowledge assistant.

Ingest: connectors to source systems (CMS, policy docs, CRM notes), chunking with semantic boundaries, enrichment with metadata (product line, region, effective date). Index: hybrid retrieval — BM25 + dense embeddings — in a vector DB (pgvector, Pinecone, or OpenSearch). Retrieve: query rewriting, metadata filtering, reranking with a cross-encoder. Generate: prompt with citations enforced, structured output where possible. Evaluate: faithfulness, context recall, answer relevance via LLM-as-judge plus a hand-labeled gold set. Govern: per-document ACLs propagated to retrieval; PII redaction at ingest.

Q3 What's the difference between fine-tuning, RAG, and prompt engineering — and how do you choose?

Prompt engineering shapes behavior with no weight changes — fastest, cheapest, first thing to try. RAG injects fresh, authoritative context at inference — best for knowledge that changes or is private. Fine-tuning changes weights — best for style, format compliance, or a narrow task on which prompts can't get to quality. Order: prompt → RAG → fine-tune. Fine-tuning is the last lever, not the first, because of cost, drift, and the need for a labeled dataset.

Q4 How do you evaluate a GenAI system rigorously?

I build an eval harness early — before scaling. Datasets: a hand-curated golden set (50–200 cases) plus a regression set grown from real traffic. Metrics: task-specific (exact match, ROUGE for summaries), RAG-specific (faithfulness, context precision/recall), behavioral (refusal rate, format compliance). Judges: deterministic checks first; LLM-as-judge with calibrated rubrics; human spot-check on a sample. CI integration: evals run on every prompt or model change, with thresholds that block regressions.

Q5 How do you control cost and latency in a production LLM app?

Cost: prompt caching for stable system prompts (huge win on Anthropic), model routing — cheap model for easy cases, escalate on uncertainty, output token limits, summarize long histories instead of resending. Latency: stream responses, parallelize independent calls, cache deterministic responses, keep retrieval under a tight budget. Observability: p50/p95/p99 latency and tokens-per-request dashboards tied to user-visible KPIs.

3 Agentic AI

Listed as "a plus" — likely a differentiator. Be ready to talk about agents vs workflows, tool use, and failure modes.

Q1 What is "Agentic AI" and how is it different from a typical LLM workflow?

A workflow is a fixed graph: deterministic steps with LLM calls at known points. An agent decides its own next step — it has tools, a goal, and a loop where it observes, plans, and acts until it stops. Agents trade predictability for flexibility. Use agents when the path can't be enumerated up front; use workflows when it can. Most production systems are workflows with small agentic sub-steps.

Q2 What are the failure modes of agents, and how do you mitigate them?

Looping (calling the same tool forever) — cap iterations and detect cycles. Drift (forgetting the goal) — re-inject the objective, summarize history. Tool misuse — strict schemas, validate args, dry-run destructive actions. Cost runaway — token + tool-call budgets per session. Prompt injection via tool output — treat tool results as untrusted, never let them rewrite the system prompt. Silent failure — log every step, replay traces in a debugger like LangSmith.

Q3 How would you design an agent for a "Client Pathway" use case — say, onboarding a new customer?

I'd not start with an agent. I'd map the pathway as a workflow first: KYC check, doc collection, eligibility, account creation, welcome comms. Then I'd identify the steps that genuinely need flexibility — e.g., handling unstructured client questions or document discrepancies — and put a small, scoped agent there with a narrow toolset (lookup, draft response, escalate). Hard guardrails: agents can read freely but write only via approved actions with human-in-the-loop on high-risk ones.

Q1 Which LLMs have you worked with and how do you choose between them?

I've used Claude (Anthropic), GPT-4 family (OpenAI), Gemini (Google), and open-weight models like Llama 3 and Mistral for on-prem use cases. 5a RAG Deep-Dive (Study Notes + Interview Q&A)

RAG = Retrieval-Augmented Generation. The model is given fresh, authoritative context at query time so it can answer questions about data it was never trained on. This is the single most common GenAI pattern in enterprise.

Study Notes — The 5 Stages of RAG

  • 1. Ingestion — pull documents from sources (SharePoint, Confluence, S3, DBs). Strip noise (headers, footers, boilerplate). Convert PDFs/HTML/DOCX to clean text. Attach metadata (source, author, date, ACL).
  • 2. Chunking — split documents into retrievable units. Too small → loss of context. Too large → noisy retrieval and wasted tokens. Typical: 500–1000 tokens with 10–20% overlap. Prefer semantic chunking (split on headings/paragraphs) over fixed-size.
  • 3. Embedding & Indexing — convert each chunk into a dense vector (e.g., text-embedding-3-large, voyage-3, bge-m3) and store in a vector DB (pgvector, Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant, OpenSearch). Index metadata for filtering.
  • 4. Retrieval — embed the query, find top-k similar chunks (k=4–10 typical). Apply metadata filters (e.g., only docs the user can see). Hybrid retrieval = dense (semantic) + sparse (BM25 keyword) merged with Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF).
  • 5. Generation — stuff retrieved chunks into the prompt as context, instruct the LLM to answer using only that context, and to cite sources. Return answer + citations.

Q1 Why use RAG instead of just fine-tuning the model on your data?

RAG separates knowledge from reasoning. Fine-tuning bakes facts into weights — they go stale, are hard to update, can't enforce ACLs, and can't be cited. RAG keeps facts in a queryable store: update a doc and the next query sees it; remove a doc and the model forgets it. You get freshness, attributability, and access control for free. Fine-tune for style and format; RAG for knowledge.

Q2 Walk me through chunking strategies and how to choose.

Fixed-size (e.g., 800 tokens, 100 overlap) — simple, fast, but cuts mid-sentence. Recursive — splits on natural separators (paragraph → sentence → word) to stay under a size cap; good default. Semantic — splits where embedding similarity drops; expensive but high quality for heterogeneous docs. Document-aware — split by headings for Markdown, by rows for tables, by function for code. Rule of thumb: start recursive @ 800/100, measure retrieval quality, only get fancier if metrics demand it.

Q3 Naive RAG often fails — what are the top failure modes?

(1) Bad retrieval — the right chunk isn't in top-k. Caused by poor chunking, weak embeddings, or query/doc vocabulary mismatch. (2) Lost in the middle — LLMs ignore context buried in long prompts. (3) Hallucination despite context — model answers from training data instead of provided context. (4) No-answer cases — model fabricates rather than saying "I don't know." (5) Stale index — docs updated but vectors not re-computed. (6) ACL leaks — user sees content they shouldn't because filters weren't applied.

Basics — Dense Vectors, Sparse Vectors & Reranking

Before hybrid search or ColBERT, you need a clear picture of what gets stored in the index and how the two-stage retrieve → rerank pipeline works.

Dense Vectors

A dense vector is a fixed-length array of floats (typically 384–3072 dimensions) where every dimension carries signal. A bi-encoder embedding model (e.g., text-embedding-3-large, bge-m3) reads a chunk of text and outputs one vector that summarizes its meaning. At query time you embed the question the same way and find the nearest vectors by cosine similarity or dot product.

  • Strengths: semantic matching — "5-year fixed mortgage" matches "five-year fixed-rate home loan" even with no shared keywords.
  • Weaknesses: misses exact IDs, product codes, acronyms, rare proper nouns. One vector per chunk = coarse representation.
  • Index: stored in a vector DB or pgvector; searched with ANN algorithms (HNSW, IVF) for speed at scale.
  • Typical dims: 384 (small models), 768 (BERT-era), 1024 (BGE-large), 1536 (OpenAI), 3072 (OpenAI large).

Sparse Vectors

A sparse vector is mostly zeros — only a few dimensions are non-zero, each tied to a vocabulary term (or learned feature). Classic keyword search (BM25, TF-IDF) is sparse retrieval without neural embeddings: "mortgage" and "ISA" hit specific term dimensions.

  • BM25 — inverted-index keyword ranking. Fast, explainable, great for SKUs, policy numbers, legal citations.
  • Learned sparse (SPLADE, uniCOIL) — a transformer expands text into a sparse weight vector over the vocabulary. Gets semantic expansion ("car" → also weights "vehicle") while staying sparse and fast on inverted indexes.
  • Why hybrid? Dense and sparse fail in opposite ways. Fuse both with Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) or weighted score blending.

Reranking — The Two-Stage Pattern

Stage 1 (retrieval): cast a wide net — top-50 or top-100 candidates via fast bi-encoder similarity, BM25, or hybrid. Stage 2 (reranking): a slower, more accurate model scores each (query, document) pair and reorders to top-5 for the LLM.

  • Bi-encoder — embed query and doc separately; similarity = dot product. O(n) over the corpus. Used for first pass.
  • Cross-encoder reranker — feed query + doc together through one transformer; outputs a single relevance score. O(k) over candidates only. Much more accurate.
  • Cost trade-off: reranking 50 docs adds ~100–300ms but is often the single biggest quality jump after hybrid search.
  • Examples: Cohere Rerank v3, bge-reranker-v2-m3, Voyage rerank, ms-marco-MiniLM-L-6-v2 (small/fast).

Intermediate — ColBERT, MRL & Hybrid Retrieval

Once basics work, these techniques improve recall and efficiency without jumping to a full custom stack.

ColBERT — Late Interaction Retrieval

ColBERT (Contextualized Late Interaction over BERT) sits between bi-encoders and cross-encoders. Instead of one vector per document, it stores a multi-vector representation — one embedding per token. At query time, each query token finds its best-matching doc token; scores are aggregated via MaxSim (sum of max similarities per query token).

  • Why it helps: finer-grained matching than a single pooled vector, but still indexable (unlike a full cross-encoder over the whole corpus).
  • Variants: ColBERTv2 (compressed token embeddings), Jina-ColBERT, ColQwen (multimodal pages).
  • Trade-off: more storage per doc (many vectors vs one), more complex indexing. Often used as a reranker or on smaller corpora.
  • Interview line: "Bi-encoder for speed, ColBERT for late interaction, cross-encoder for final rerank."

MRL — Matryoshka Representation Learning

MRL trains embeddings so the first N dimensions of a vector are still meaningful when you truncate. A 3072-d embedding from text-embedding-3-large can be cut to 1536 or 512 dims with modest quality loss — like Russian nesting dolls where inner layers still work.

  • Storage savings: store 512-d instead of 3072-d → ~6× less disk and RAM.
  • Latency: smaller vectors = faster distance computation and smaller HNSW graphs.
  • Multi-resolution: same model, different dims per use case — 1536 for quality-critical, 256 for high-volume dedup.
  • Models with MRL: OpenAI embedding-3 family, Nomic Embed, some BGE variants. Always validate truncation on your eval set.

Qdrant — Vector DB Deep-Dive

Qdrant is a Rust-native vector database built for filtered hybrid search at scale. Strong choice when you outgrow pgvector or need native sparse + dense in one engine.

  • Collections & points — a collection holds points; each point has an ID, one or more named vectors, and a JSON payload (metadata).
  • Named vectors — store dense (1536-d) and sparse (SPLADE) on the same point; query both in one request.
  • Payload filtering — apply ACL/region/date filters inside the HNSW search (pre-filter or post-filter). Critical for banking ACL enforcement.
  • HNSW tuning — m (edges per node), ef_construct (build quality), ef (search recall). Higher = better recall, more RAM and slower queries.
  • Quantization — scalar or product quantization compresses vectors (e.g., 4× RAM reduction) with configurable accuracy trade-off.
  • Multitenancy — shard by tenant; use payload indexes on hot filter fields (tenant_id, region).
  • When to pick Qdrant over pgvector: >10M vectors, need hybrid sparse+dense natively, heavy metadata filtering, dedicated ops team. Stay on pgvector if you're <5M docs and already on Postgres.

Learned Sparse Vectors (SPLADE) — Advanced Hybrid

SPLADE (and successors like SPLADE-v3, uniCOIL) train a transformer to output sparse term weights. You get BM25-like inverted-index speed with neural term expansion. Qdrant, Elasticsearch, and OpenSearch all support sparse vector fields. Pair with dense in RRF for state-of-the-art hybrid on BEIR-style benchmarks.

ColBERT in Production

  • Indexing: store token embeddings per chunk (often 100–300 vectors × 128-d each after compression in ColBERTv2).
  • Retrieval: MaxSim scoring — for each query token, find max dot-product with any doc token, sum across query tokens.
  • Placement: use as a second-stage retriever after dense top-200, or as a reranker before cross-encoder. Rarely the only retrieval method at >1M docs without dedicated infra (RAGatouille, Vespa).
  • Multimodal: ColPali / ColQwen apply the same late-interaction idea to page images — strong for layout-heavy PDFs.

Q1 Explain Contextual Retrieval and why it works.

Standard chunking embeds each chunk in isolation — but a chunk like "the rate is 4.5%" is meaningless without knowing it's about a 30-year fixed mortgage in the 2024 product guide. Contextual Retrieval uses an LLM (cheaply, with prompt caching on the full doc) to generate a short situating context for each chunk and prepends it before embedding and BM25 indexing. The chunk now retrieves correctly because its embedding reflects its true meaning. Anthropic reported ~49% reduction in retrieval failures combined with reranking.

Q2 When would you use GraphRAG over vector RAG?

GraphRAG shines on global, multi-hop questions — "summarize the top risks across all 200 audit reports" or "how is entity X connected to entity Y across these documents?" Vector RAG retrieves local semantic neighbors and struggles with these. GraphRAG extracts entities/relations into a graph, clusters into communities, and pre-summarizes each community. Cost is much higher up front (LLM calls per document for extraction), so use it when query patterns justify it.

Q3 What's reranking and why is it worth the latency?

Embedding similarity is a bi-encoder — query and doc embedded independently. Fast but loses cross-attention nuance. A cross-encoder reranker takes (query, doc) pairs together and outputs a relevance score, capturing fine-grained interaction. Pattern: retrieve top-50 fast (bi-encoder), rerank to top-5 (cross-encoder). Adds 100–300ms but often the single biggest quality jump in the pipeline.

Q4 What's HyDE and when does it help?

HyDE (Hypothetical Document Embeddings): the LLM generates a hypothetical answer to the question, then you embed and retrieve based on that synthetic answer rather than the original question. Helps when the question and the document corpus use very different language — short questions vs. long technical docs. Adds an LLM call to the critical path, so weigh latency.

Q5 Dense vs sparse vectors — when do you need both?

Dense vectors capture semantic meaning — paraphrases, synonyms, conceptual similarity. Sparse vectors (BM25 or learned SPLADE) capture lexical overlap — product codes, legal references, acronyms, exact names. In banking RAG you almost always need both: dense finds "early repayment charge" when the user says "pay off mortgage early fee"; sparse finds "SKU-88421" or "FCA CONC 5.2.1R". Fuse with RRF; don't rely on dense alone.

Q6 Explain ColBERT and how it differs from bi-encoder and cross-encoder.

A bi-encoder pools the whole document into one vector — fast but coarse. A cross-encoder runs query and doc through shared attention — accurate but too slow for millions of docs. ColBERT stores one vector per token in the document and scores via MaxSim: each query token matches its best doc token, scores are summed. You get token-level interaction without scoring every (query, doc) pair at index time. Typical pattern: bi-encoder top-200 → ColBERT rescore → cross-encoder rerank top-5.

Q7 What is MRL and why would you truncate embedding dimensions?

Matryoshka Representation Learning (MRL) trains embeddings so lower-dimensional prefixes remain useful. Truncating 1536-d → 512-d cuts storage and speeds up HNSW search with ~5–15% recall loss (model-dependent). Use it when you have millions of vectors and RAM/disk is the bottleneck, or when you want one model serving both a high-quality tier (full dims) and a fast dedup tier (256-d). Always measure on your gold set — don't truncate blindly.

Q8 Why Qdrant over pgvector or Pinecone?

pgvector wins when you're <5–10M vectors, already on Postgres, and want one database. Qdrant wins when you need native hybrid sparse+dense on the same point, heavy payload filtering inside HNSW, quantization at scale, or >10M vectors with dedicated indexing. Pinecone wins when you want fully managed with minimal ops. For regulated banking: Qdrant self-hosted or Qdrant Cloud with private networking gives you ACL payload filters and audit-friendly point-level metadata.

Q9 When do small embedding/reranking models beat frontier APIs?

Small models (bge-small, all-MiniLM, ms-marco-MiniLM reranker) win on cost, latency, and data residency — self-host on CPU, no per-token bill, data never leaves your VPC. They lose on multilingual coverage, domain jargon, and absolute recall. Rule: start with a small open model + your eval set; if recall@10 on the gold set is >85%, ship it. If not, step up to bge-m3 or voyage-3 for embeddings and bge-reranker-v2 or Cohere Rerank for the rerank stage. Use small LLMs (Phi-3-mini, Haiku) only for routing/rewrite — not final regulated answers.

Stage 1 — Data Sourcing & Cleaning

  • Sources — SharePoint (15k policy docs), Confluence (8k pages), Product DB (Postgres), Salesforce KB articles, Outlook FAQs.

  • PII handling — Microsoft Presidio or AWS Comprehend to detect PAN, IBAN, NI numbers, names. Redact if not needed for the use case; tokenize if needed for retrieval.

Stage 2 — Labeling & Metadata Enrichment

  • Why label? Metadata enables filtering ("only mortgage docs valid in 2026"), routing (commercial vs retail), and ACL enforcement.
  • What to label:
    • Document-level: doc_type (policy / FAQ / product sheet), product_line, region, effective_date, expiry_date, sensitivity (Public / Internal / Confidential / Restricted), owner.
    • Chunk-level: section heading, applies_to (customer segment), regulatory_ref (e.g., "FCA CONC 5.2").
  • How:
    • Rule-based extractors for structured fields (filename patterns, doc properties).
    • LLM-assisted labeling for semantic fields (doc_type, applies_to) — sample, verify with SMEs, then scale.
    • SME-in-the-loop for the gold set: 200 docs reviewed by compliance + product SMEs to anchor the labeling rubric and serve as eval ground truth.
    • Tooling: Label Studio or Argilla for the SME workflow; annotators agree on a rubric before labeling at volume.
  • ACL propagation — every chunk inherits the source doc's permission set. Retrieval applies the user's identity as a filter before similarity search.

Stage 3 — Chunking, Embedding & Indexing

  • Chunking — recursive splitter at 800 tokens / 100 overlap, with semantic boundary respect (don't break mid-table, mid-list).
  • Contextual prefix — prepend 1–2 sentences of doc context to each chunk before embedding (Anthropic's Contextual Retrieval). Use prompt caching on the full doc to keep this cheap.
  • Embedding model — start with text-embedding-3-large (1536-d) or voyage-3. Evaluate against domain on the gold set; consider domain-tuned embeddings (FinBERT-style) only if measurable lift.
  • Indexes — pgvector (HNSW index) for dense; OpenSearch for BM25; both keyed on chunk_id with metadata. Reconcile via RRF at query time.
  • Refresh strategy — incremental: CDC events trigger re-embed of changed chunks. Full rebuild quarterly. Track index_version; queries pin a version for reproducibility.

Stage 4 — Retrieval & Generation

  • Query pipeline:
    1. Auth → resolve user identity, roles, regions.
    2. Query rewrite (LLM, fast/cheap model) to expand abbreviations, resolve "this/that" against chat history.
    3. Hybrid retrieval (top-50 dense + top-50 BM25) with hard filters: ACL, region, effective_date <= today <= expiry_date.
    4. RRF fusion → top-30.
    5. Cross-encoder rerank → top-5.
    6. LLM (Claude) with system prompt enforcing: "answer only from context; cite [n]; if unsure, say so."
    7. Post-processor verifies citations exist, redacts any leaked PII, logs full trace.
  • Guardrails — input filter for prompt injection & off-topic; output filter for PII leakage, toxic content, claim-without-citation. Block if violated; log for review.

5d Evaluation: With and Without RAGAS

Evaluation is what separates a demo from a production system. Know the metrics by name, what they measure, and how to compute them with and without a framework.

Study Notes — The Two Halves of RAG Eval

  • Retrieval eval — did we get the right chunks? (Independent of the LLM.)
  • Generation eval — given those chunks, is the answer good?

Always measure both — a great answer with bad retrieval is luck; bad answer with great retrieval is a prompt/model problem.

Core Metrics (Definitions)

MetricWhat it measuresHow
Hit@kWas at least one expected source in top-k?Compare retrieved IDs vs ground-truth IDs.
MRRMean Reciprocal Rank — how high was the first correct hit?1/rank of first relevant doc, averaged.
nDCG@kQuality of ranking, with graded relevance and position discountStandard IR metric; needs graded labels.
FaithfulnessAre all claims in the answer supported by the context?Decompose answer into claims; for each, check entailment from context. (LLM-judged.)
Answer RelevanceDoes the answer actually address the question?LLM generates N questions from the answer; measure cosine similarity to original question.
Context PrecisionOf the retrieved chunks, how many are actually relevant? (Are we sending noise to the LLM?)Per chunk, LLM-judge "is this relevant to the question?" → ranked precision.
Context RecallOf the info needed, how much was retrieved? (Did we miss something?)Decompose ground-truth answer into claims; check each is supported by retrieved context.
Answer CorrectnessDoes the answer match the ground-truth answer?Combination of factual overlap + semantic similarity.

What is RAGAS?

RAGAS (Retrieval-Augmented Generation Assessment) is an open-source Python framework that implements the metrics above out of the box. It uses an LLM as a judge for faithfulness, relevance, context precision/recall, and answer correctness. Reference-free where possible (faithfulness needs no ground truth) — reference-based where required (context recall and answer correctness need ground-truth answers).

Why use it: standard, comparable, fast to set up, integrates with LangChain/LlamaIndex.
Why not (or augment): LLM-judge introduces its own bias; metrics are aggregate, not always diagnostic; you may need domain-specific metrics it doesn't ship.

Evaluating WITHOUT RAGAS — Roll-Your-Own

When you can't use RAGAS (regulated environment, custom domain metrics, judge-model bias concerns), compute the same ideas yourself. It's not magic — it's structured LLM judging plus deterministic checks.

from anthropic import Anthropic
import json

judge = Anthropic()

def faithfulness_score(answer: str, contexts: list[str]) -> float:
    """Decompose answer into atomic claims; check each is supported."""
    claims = decompose_into_claims(answer, judge)  # LLM call
    supported = 0
    joined_ctx = "\n\n".join(contexts)
    for claim in claims:
        msg = judge.messages.create(
            model="claude-haiku-4-5-20251001",
            max_tokens=10,
            system="Reply only YES or NO.",
            messages=[{"role": "user", "content":
                f"Is this claim supported by the context?\n\n"
                f"CONTEXT:\n{joined_ctx}\n\nCLAIM: {claim}"}],
        )
        if msg.content[0].text.strip().upper().startswith("YES"):
            supported += 1
    return supported / max(len(claims), 1)

def hit_at_k(retrieved_ids: list[str], expected_ids: list[str], k: int = 5) -> int:
    return int(any(rid in expected_ids for rid in retrieved_ids[:k]))

def mrr(retrieved_ids: list[str], expected_ids: list[str]) -> float:
    for i, rid in enumerate(retrieved_ids, 1):
        if rid in expected_ids:
            return 1.0 / i
    return 0.0

def citation_validity(answer: str, allowed_sources: set[str]) -> bool:
    cited = extract_citations(answer)  # regex on [n] markers → source IDs
    return all(c in allowed_sources for c in cited)

# Aggregate over the gold set
def run_eval(gold_set, system):
    results = []
    for row in gold_set:
        out = system(row["question"], user_id=row["user_id"])
        results.append({
            "hit@5": hit_at_k(out.retrieved_ids, row["expected_ids"]),
            "mrr": mrr(out.retrieved_ids, row["expected_ids"]),
            "faithfulness": faithfulness_score(out.answer, out.contexts),
            "citations_ok": citation_validity(out.answer, set(out.retrieved_ids)),
        })
    return aggregate(results)

Q1 Walk me through how you evaluate a RAG system end-to-end.

Two halves: retrieval and generation. Retrieval — Hit@k, MRR, nDCG against a gold set of (question, expected source IDs). Generation — Faithfulness, Answer Relevance, Context Precision/Recall using RAGAS or my own LLM-judge harness. I run these in CI on every prompt/model change with blocking thresholds. In production, I sample 5% of live traffic for online faithfulness scoring and watch for drift, plus implicit signals like thumbs and reformulation rates.

Q2 What are RAGAS's limitations and how do you compensate?

Judge-model bias — same model family judging itself inflates scores. I cross-check with a different judge (e.g., Claude judges GPT, GPT judges Claude). Aggregate scores hide failures — I slice by intent, doc type, user segment to find weak spots. Doesn't capture domain truth — for banking I add deterministic checks (numerical exact-match on rates and dates, regulatory citation correctness) that no general framework can give. Cost — LLM-as-judge is expensive at scale, so I sample.

Q3 How do you build a gold/eval dataset?

Seed with real production queries (anonymized) — 200–500 covering the intent distribution. Curate ideal answers and expected sources with SMEs. Stratify across intents, doc types, and difficulty (easy / multi-hop / out-of-scope). Grow the set monthly with regression cases from production failures. Version it in git. Hold out a sealed set the team can't see during prompt iteration to prevent overfitting.

Q4 How do you monitor a deployed RAG system?

System health: latency p50/p95/p99, error rate, tokens, cost. Quality online: sample-based LLM-judge faithfulness and relevance, citation validity rate, refusal-rate on out-of-scope. Implicit feedback: thumbs, copy rate, escalation rate, query reformulation rate. Drift: embedding distribution shift on incoming queries, top-k composition shift, new doc-type distribution. Alerts on threshold breaches, with feedback feeding the gold set and doc remediation queue weekly.

Interview soundbite: "I treat eval as a first-class product surface — you can't ship what you can't measure, and you can't improve a RAG system on vibes."

5e LangChain v1 Agents & LangGraph Workflows

Two patterns you must be able to write live: an agent using LangChain v1's create_agent(), and a graph workflow using LangGraph (no agent — just deterministic nodes and edges). Know the difference and when to choose each.

Study Notes — Agent vs Workflow

Agent (LangChain create_agent)Workflow (LangGraph StateGraph)
Control flowLLM decides next step (tool-call loop)You define the graph; deterministic edges
When to useOpen-ended tasks; path can't be enumeratedKnown steps; you want predictability
Cost / latencyVariable, often higher (multiple LLM calls)Predictable; fewer LLM calls
DebuggabilityHarder — model decisions hiddenEasy — every transition is in code
Production fitUse sparingly, with guardrails & HITLDefault for most enterprise GenAI

Mental model: agents are workflows where the edges are decided by an LLM. Most real systems are workflows with a small agentic sub-step (e.g., a research node that's an agent), not end-to-end agents.

Key LangChain v1 Concepts (Memorize)

  • create_agent() — the canonical way to build an agent. Replaces older AgentExecutor and create_react_agent. Handles the tool-call loop for you.
  • @tool — decorator that turns a Python function into an LLM-callable tool. The docstring becomes the tool description; type hints become the schema.
  • checkpointer — persistence layer (e.g., MemorySaver, Postgres). Keyed by thread_id for multi-turn memory.
  • middleware — intercepts the agent loop. Used for human-in-the-loop approval, custom logging, retries, dynamic prompt rewriting.
  • response_format — Pydantic model for structured output; agent returns typed object in result["structured_response"].
  • recursion_limit — pass in invoke config to cap loop iterations (prevents runaway agents).

Example 1 — Agentic System (LangChain v1) for Banking Support

    Args:

    """

    action_taken: str = Field(description="What the agent did.")
    next_steps: list[str] = Field(default_factory=list)

    middleware=[hitl],

)

)

print(result["structured_response"].next_steps)

Example 2 — Simple LangGraph Workflow (No Agent): Loan Triage

Linear pipeline with conditional routing. Classify a loan application → route to fast-track, manual-review, or auto-decline. No LLM autonomy — every transition is in code.

from typing import Literal
from typing_extensions import TypedDict
from langgraph.graph import StateGraph, START, END

class LoanState(TypedDict):
    application_id: str
    credit_score: int
    income: float
    requested_amount: float
    decision: str
    rationale: str

def classify(state: LoanState) -> dict:
    score, income, amt = state["credit_score"], state["income"], state["requested_amount"]
    dti = amt / max(income, 1)
    if score >= 740 and dti < 3:
        bucket = "fast_track"
    elif score < 580 or dti > 6:
        bucket = "auto_decline"
    else:
        bucket = "manual_review"
    return {"decision": bucket}

def route(state: LoanState) -> Literal["fast_track", "manual_review", "auto_decline"]:
    return state["decision"]

def fast_track(state: LoanState) -> dict:
    return {"rationale": "Approved on bureau score and low DTI."}

def manual_review(state: LoanState) -> dict:
    return {"rationale": "Routed to underwriter queue for review."}

def auto_decline(state: LoanState) -> dict:
    return {"rationale": "Declined: bureau or DTI thresholds breached."}

graph = (
    StateGraph(LoanState)
    .add_node("classify", classify)
    .add_node("fast_track", fast_track)
    .add_node("manual_review", manual_review)
    .add_node("auto_decline", auto_decline)
    .add_edge(START, "classify")
    .add_conditional_edges("classify", route,
                           ["fast_track", "manual_review", "auto_decline"])
    .add_edge("fast_track", END)
    .add_edge("manual_review", END)
    .add_edge("auto_decline", END)
    .compile()
)

result = graph.invoke({
    "application_id": "APP-1001",
    "credit_score": 760, "income": 90000, "requested_amount": 200000,
    "decision": "", "rationale": "",
})
print(result["decision"], "—", result["rationale"])

Example 3 — LangGraph RAG Workflow (No Agent)

Deterministic RAG pipeline as a graph: retrieve → grade context → generate (or refuse). No model autonomy; you control every step. This is the shape of most production RAG systems.

from typing import Literal, Annotated
import operator
from typing_extensions import TypedDict
from langgraph.graph import StateGraph, START, END
from langchain_anthropic import ChatAnthropic

llm = ChatAnthropic(model="claude-opus-4-7", temperature=0)

class RAGState(TypedDict):
    question: str
    contexts: list[str]
    context_ok: bool
    answer: str
    citations: Annotated[list[str], operator.add]

def retrieve(state: RAGState) -> dict:
    chunks = vector_search(state["question"], k=5)  # your retriever
    return {"contexts": [c.text for c in chunks],
            "citations": [c.source for c in chunks]}

def grade_context(state: RAGState) -> dict:
    """LLM judges whether the retrieved context can actually answer the question."""
    judgement = llm.invoke(
        f"Can the question be answered from this context? Reply YES or NO.\n\n"
        f"Q: {state['question']}\nCONTEXT:\n" + "\n---\n".join(state["contexts"])
    ).content.strip().upper()
    return {"context_ok": judgement.startswith("YES")}

def route_after_grade(state: RAGState) -> Literal["generate", "refuse"]:
    return "generate" if state["context_ok"] else "refuse"

def generate(state: RAGState) -> dict:
    ctx = "\n\n".join(f"[{i+1}] {c}" for i, c in enumerate(state["contexts"]))
    msg = llm.invoke(
        f"Answer ONLY from the context. Cite sources as [n].\n\n"
        f"Context:\n{ctx}\n\nQuestion: {state['question']}"
    )
    return {"answer": msg.content}

def refuse(state: RAGState) -> dict:
    return {"answer": "I don't have enough information to answer that confidently."}

graph = (
    StateGraph(RAGState)
    .add_node("retrieve", retrieve)
    .add_node("grade", grade_context)
    .add_node("generate", generate)
    .add_node("refuse", refuse)
    .add_edge(START, "retrieve")
    .add_edge("retrieve", "grade")
    .add_conditional_edges("grade", route_after_grade, ["generate", "refuse"])
    .add_edge("generate", END)
    .add_edge("refuse", END)
    .compile()
)

result = graph.invoke({"question": "What's the ERC on a 5-year fixed mortgage?",
                       "contexts": [], "context_ok": False,
                       "answer": "", "citations": []})
print(result["answer"], "\nSources:", result["citations"])

Example 4 — LangGraph Parallel Fan-Out (No Agent)

Process a batch of client documents in parallel using the Send API. Useful for evaluation pipelines, bulk summarization, or any embarrassingly-parallel work.

from typing import Annotated
import operator
from typing_extensions import TypedDict
from langgraph.graph import StateGraph, START, END
from langgraph.types import Send

class BatchState(TypedDict):
    documents: list[str]
    summaries: Annotated[list[str], operator.add]   # reducer accumulates
    report: str

def fan_out(state: BatchState):
    # Returns a list of Send commands — one worker per document.
    return [Send("summarize", {"doc": d}) for d in state["documents"]]

def summarize(state: dict) -> dict:
    # Each worker runs in parallel.
    return {"summaries": [f"Summary of: {state['doc'][:30]}..."]}

def report(state: BatchState) -> dict:
    return {"report": f"Processed {len(state['summaries'])} documents."}

graph = (
    StateGraph(BatchState)
    .add_node("summarize", summarize)
    .add_node("report", report)
    .add_conditional_edges(START, fan_out, ["summarize"])
    .add_edge("summarize", "report")
    .add_edge("report", END)
    .compile()
)

result = graph.invoke({
    "documents": ["doc A text...", "doc B text...", "doc C text..."],
    "summaries": [], "report": "",
})
print(result["report"])
Q1 When do you choose create_agent vs a hand-built LangGraph workflow?

I default to a workflow because it's predictable, debuggable, and cheaper. I reach for an agent only when the path genuinely can't be enumerated — open-ended research, tool-rich problem solving — and even then I scope it tightly with a small toolset, recursion limits, and HITL on any destructive action. Most production systems I build are workflows with one agentic node, not the other way around.

Q2 What's the role of middleware in LangChain v1?

Middleware sits inside the agent loop and intercepts tool calls or LLM steps. The most important built-in is HumanInTheLoopMiddleware, which pauses execution before specified tools fire and resumes via Command(resume=...) after a human decides. You can also write custom middleware with @wrap_tool_call for logging, redaction, or dynamic prompt rewriting. Middleware is how you add enterprise controls without rewriting the agent.

Q3 What's a reducer in LangGraph and why does it matter?

A reducer defines how concurrent updates to a state field are merged. Default behavior is "last write wins" — fine for scalars but disastrous for lists when nodes run in parallel (Send fan-out). Annotating a list with operator.add makes updates concatenate instead. Forgetting reducers on accumulator fields is the single most common LangGraph bug.

Q4 How do you prevent an agent from looping forever or burning budget?

Three controls: (1) recursion_limit in invoke config — hard cap on loop iterations. (2) Tool design — make tools idempotent, return clear "done" signals, validate args strictly. (3) Token / cost budget per session enforced in middleware — kill the run if it exceeds the cap and surface the partial result. I also log every step to LangSmith so I can replay and tune.

5 SDLC & Enterprise Software Engineering

Q1 What does "enterprise-grade" mean to you in a GenAI context?

Enterprise-grade means the system survives Monday morning — not just the demo. Concretely: (1) reproducibility — pinned models, prompts in code, deterministic eval; (2) observability — every LLM call is traceable end-to-end with cost and latency; (3) security & compliance — data classification, PII handling, audit logs, SOC2/ISO controls; (4) change management — prompt and model changes go through CI, code review, and staged rollout; (5) operational readiness — runbooks, on-call, SLOs.

Prompts are code. They live in the repo, are reviewed in PRs, and have unit tests (deterministic checks) and eval tests (regression on a golden set). Trunk-based with short-lived feature branches and feature flags for incomplete work. Required CI: lint, type check, unit tests, contract tests, eval gate. Releases are continuous to staging, gated to prod. For models: shadow-mode first (compare new vs old offline), then canary, then full rollout, with automatic rollback on eval/SLO breach.

6 Cloud-native & Modern Architectures

Q1 What does "cloud-native" mean beyond "running on AWS"?

It means the system is designed to exploit cloud properties: elasticity (autoscaling on real signal, not CPU), statelessness at the compute layer with state pushed to managed services, declarative infrastructure (Terraform/Pulumi, GitOps), observability as a first-class concern, and resilience patterns (retries, circuit breakers, graceful degradation). Containers and Kubernetes are common but not required — Lambda + Step Functions can be just as cloud-native.

Q2 How would you architect a GenAI service for global, low-latency clients?

Edge-terminate TLS; route to the nearest region. Stateless app tier behind a load balancer; LLM provider chosen with a region-aware policy (data residency first, then latency). RAG indexes replicated regionally with eventual consistency on the write side. Async job queue (SQS/PubSub) for long-running generations. Caching at three layers: CDN for static, Redis for session, prompt-cache at the model layer.

Q3 Microservices vs modular monolith for a transformation initiative?

Default to a modular monolith until you have a real reason to split — team boundaries, scaling asymmetry, or independent release cadence. Microservices add network, observability, and data-consistency tax that kills early-stage velocity. I've seen more transformations stall on premature microservices than on monoliths.

7 Databases & Data Integration

Q1 Walk me through choosing a data store for a GenAI knowledge base.

Start by classifying the queries: vector similarity (semantic search), structured filters (metadata, ACLs), full-text (keyword), graph (relationships). For most enterprise cases, Postgres with pgvector + BM25 (or OpenSearch) covers 80%. Move to a dedicated vector DB only when you cross tens of millions of vectors or need specialized index types. For graph-heavy domains (entity linking across client records), Neo4j or a property graph in Postgres.

Q2 What integration patterns have you used between AI services and systems of record?

Event-driven for asynchronous, decoupled flows — CDC from the SoR into a Kafka topic, AI enrichment service consumes and writes derived data back. Synchronous APIs for user-facing flows where freshness matters. Batch ETL for index hydration. Key patterns: idempotent consumers, schema registry, dead-letter queues, and a clear data contract between teams.

Q3 How do you handle PII and sensitive data in GenAI pipelines?

Classify at ingest — every doc gets a sensitivity label. Redact obvious PII before indexing if it's not needed for the use case. Filter at retrieval using ACLs propagated from source. Avoid sending to third-party models when classification forbids it — route to an on-prem or zero-retention endpoint. Audit every prompt and response with hashes for forensics.

9 Behavioral & Leadership (AVP-level)

At AVP level, technical chops are table stakes. They're hiring for judgement, influence, and ability to drive transformation.

Q1 Tell me about a time you drove a transformation initiative.
S — what was the problem and the org context.
T — your specific charter and constraints.
A — how you built coalition, the technical approach, the rollout plan, how you handled resistance.
R — quantified outcome (cycle time, cost, NPS, adoption %), plus what you'd do differently.
Q2 Tell me about a time you disagreed with a senior stakeholder.

Lead with respect for the disagreement, show you sought to understand their position first, then explain how you presented data/evidence, the compromise reached, and the outcome. Never tell a story where you "won" by being louder. Tell one where you changed minds with rigor — or where you were the one who changed your mind.

Q3 How do you operate independently while collaborating cross-functionally?

Independence ≠ isolation. I drive my own priorities and unblock myself, but I make my work visible — short written updates, shared docs, decisions logged. With cross-functional partners (product, risk, legal, ops), I co-design outcomes early so I'm not negotiating in PRs. I optimize for fewer, higher-quality syncs over constant standups.

Q4 How do you communicate technical concepts to non-technical executives?

Lead with the outcome and the decision being asked of them. Then one slide of "how it works" using their language — analogies to systems they already understand. Then risks and tradeoffs in plain terms. End with what success looks like and what you need from them. Avoid jargon; never explain by listing technologies.

Q5 Tell me about a failure.

Pick a real one — owned, not blamed on others. Show what you learned and how it changed your approach since. AVP-level interviewers can smell a sanitized "my biggest weakness is I work too hard" answer at fifty paces.

More behavioral prompts
  • A time you had to make a decision with incomplete information.
  • A time you mentored or up-skilled a team on a new technology.
  • A time you said no to a stakeholder request.
  • A time you killed your own project because it wasn't working.
  • How do you prioritize when everything feels urgent?

11 Questions YOU Should Ask Them

At AVP level, the questions you ask signal seniority as much as your answers.

  • Strategy: "What does 'transformation' look like in concrete terms over the next 12 months for Client Pathways? What's the leading indicator the org watches?"
  • Scope: "How is this role's mandate balanced between hands-on engineering and influencing across teams? What's the expected mix?"
  • Org: "Who owns the AI platform vs who consumes it? How is governance structured today?"
  • Maturity: "What GenAI use cases are in production today? What's the most painful lesson the team has learned?"
  • Constraints: "What are the data residency and model-vendor constraints I'd be working within?"
  • Success: "What would 'great' look like 6 months in? What about 'failed' — what would that look like?"
  • Stakeholders: "Who are the three people I'd need to win over in the first quarter, and what do they care about?"
  • Investment: "How is the budget for emerging tech allocated — central, charge-back, or business-unit funded?"
